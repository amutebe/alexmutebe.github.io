Introduction to Machine Learning (ML)

Machine learning has become an integral part of our daily life. From browsing on internet through to getting a bank loan, machine learning (ML) is used to make decisions and for profiling. The availability of big data on a real-time basis facilitates the learning of the ML algorithms, which in turn provides machines with the ability to make more accurate decisions autonomously (Schwab & Zahid 2020).

The discussion on Klaus Schwab’s 2016 article from the World Economic Forum introduced me to the profound implications of Industry 4.0. In the context of the Introduction to Machine Learning (ML) module, this discussion was particularly relevant as it highlighted the transformative role of ML in the era of the Fourth Industrial Revolution. Here, I pointed out the significant influence of industrial revolutions on society, particularly Industry 4.0’s role in advancing industrial workplaces and addressing their challenges. I highlighted the transformation of the healthcare industry through the integration of digital, biological, and physical domains. I however mentioned that the reliance on technology has its drawbacks, as evidenced by the COVID-19 pandemic, which revealed the limitations of technology in crisis prevention and management. The pandemic’s impact on the health sector underscores the need for comprehensive solutions, including sound policies and accessible healthcare, to address global challenges effectively.

Armed with the knowledge from the module and the insights from Schwab’s article, I am motivated to explore the intersection of ML and healthcare further. My goal is to contribute to the development of predictive models that can enhance diagnostic accuracy and aid in the early detection of diseases. Reflecting on the ethical considerations discussed, I am also committed to ensuring that the deployment of such technologies in healthcare is done responsibly and equitably.
