Interviews, Survey Methods, and Questionnaire Design

In unit 5, The module introduced me to various research techniques. In-depth interviews allowed me to explore participants’ perspectives deeply, while surveys provided a broader view of opinions and trends. I also learned that questionnaires serve as the backbone of surveys, containing carefully crafted questions to collect relevant data. Initially, I felt a mix of excitement and nervousness. Interviews seemed daunting—what if I asked the wrong questions? Surveys, on the other hand, felt more accessible due to their online nature. This module expanded my toolkit as a researcher. I now appreciate the synergy between interviews, surveys, and questionnaires. Each method has its place, and ethical considerations guide our choices. As I move forward, I’ll apply these insights to real-world research projects.

Reflective Activity

Case Study: Inappropriate Use of Surveys

The reflecting activity looked at the unconsented access to millions of Facebook users' personal data by political consultancy firm Cambridge Analytica. They used an innocuous-looking survey app to collect information, which allowed them to build detailed profiles and influence political campaigns. The data obtained through surveys helped Cambridge Analytica create targeted political advertisements and influence voters’ opinions. Ethically, Cambridge Analytica violated users’ privacy rights and trust. Socially undermined democratic processes by manipulating public opinion. From the legal perspective, it led to investigations, fines, and increased scrutiny of data practices. This action damaged the reputation of data analytics and research firms. Similar examples include Online Quizzes and Personality Tests. Some seemingly harmless quizzes collect personal data (e.g., favourite colour, birthdate) and sell it to advertisers or use it for targeted. Also, misleading health surveys may lead individuals to make poor health decisions based on inaccurate information.
